# Good Strategy Bad Strategy

Most strategy is bad strategy. Not because strategists are dumb—because strategy requires choices, and choices create enemies. Organizations optimize for avoiding conflict. The result: "strategies" that are negotiated lists, financial targets, or vision statements with no teeth.

Bad strategy is the default. The document that acknowledges no hard choices, names no obstacles, rules out no options—that's what organizations produce when left alone. Good strategy is rare because it requires what organizations resist: honest diagnosis, genuine focus, and the willingness to be wrong about something specific.

---

## When to Use This

**Evaluating something called "strategy":**
- Someone hands you a strategy document to review
- You're reading a roadmap, initiative, or "strategic plan"
- Leadership presented "the strategy" and something feels off
- OKRs or annual plans are being called strategy

**When you suspect it's not actually strategy:**
- The document is a list where nothing was cut
- Everyone's priority made it in
- There's no diagnosis—just goals and initiatives
- You can't identify what hard choice was made

**When building strategy from scratch:**
- You need to define strategy for a product, team, or company
- You're drowning in challenges and need to find the crux
- You have a vague sense of direction but no kernel
- Someone asks "what's our strategy?" and you can't answer crisply

**The trigger:** If you can't state the diagnosis in two sentences, you don't have strategy yet.

---

## The Principles

These apply across all modes. Strategy is focus, and focus is rare.

### The kernel

Good strategy has three parts. Only three.

**Diagnosis:** What's actually going on? Not symptoms—the underlying dynamic. Not "sales are down" but *why* sales are down. The diagnosis should make people uncomfortable. If everyone nods easily, you've named the problem everyone's willing to acknowledge—not the real one.

**Guiding policy:** An approach that addresses the diagnosis. Not a goal—a method. "Focus on enterprise" is policy. "Be number one" is not. Good policy creates clarity by ruling things out.

**Coherent action:** Specific steps that implement the policy. *Coherent* means they reinforce each other. If sales pushes volume while product builds premium, you don't have strategy—you have political compromise.

### Bad strategy happens on purpose

Organizations produce bad strategy reliably because:

**Strategy requires saying no.** Focus on X means not-Y. Not-Y has a champion with power. So organizations say yes to everything and call it strategy.

**Diagnosis requires honesty that gets punished.** Real problems implicate people. The CEO's pet project. The failed acquisition. Naming the problem means naming names.

**Strategy bets on an unproven thesis.** If it fails, someone's responsible. Vague goals keep everyone safe.

When you see bad strategy, ask: *what conflict was this designed to avoid?*

### Leverage and focus

Strategy finds points where focused effort creates disproportionate results. Spread effort thin and nothing breaks through. Five priorities means none.

The strategy with the tightest focus usually wins—not because focus is magic, but because focus is rare. Everyone else is spreading themselves thin.

## When Activated

Determine the mode from context or by asking:

- **Diagnose mode** — "I have a strategy/plan/doc. Is it real strategy?"
- **Build mode** — "I need to create actual strategy for something."
- **Crux mode** — "I'm overwhelmed with challenges. Help me find the one that matters."

---

## Diagnose Mode: Testing for Real Strategy

You have something called a strategy. Let's find out if it is one.

### The five-minute smell test

Run these in order. Most "strategies" fail in the first two minutes:

**1. Where's the diagnosis?**

Does this document name the specific challenge it addresses? Not symptoms—the obstacle. If you can't find a crisp statement of the problem, there's no strategy.

*Red flag:* The document jumps straight to goals and initiatives. Ask them "what's the hard problem this solves?" and watch them flounder.

**2. Is it a list?**

Count the strategic priorities. Count the initiatives. Did each stakeholder get something included? Was anything cut?

*Red flag:* Nothing was removed. That's not strategy—that's appeasement.

**3. Scan for blue-sky language.**

"Leverage synergies." "Customer-centric innovation." "Best-in-class execution." Translation: nothing was decided.

*Test:* Ask them to say it in plain English. If they can't, it's fog.

**4. Goals without a how.**

"Our strategy is 15% revenue growth" is not strategy. That's a target. Strategy is the specific insight that lets you achieve it when competitors want the same thing.

**5. What hard choice was made?**

What does this strategy say you *won't* do? If the answer is nothing—if everything's still on the table—it's not strategy.

### Diagnostic output

Present findings as:

1. **The verdict:** Real strategy or not? (Be direct.)
2. **What's missing:** Which elements of the kernel are absent or weak?
3. **The avoided conflict:** What hard choice did this document sidestep?
4. **If salvageable:** What honest diagnosis would need to be named?

## Build Mode: Constructing the Kernel

You need actual strategy. Let's build it from the ground up.

### The scaffolding questions

Before writing anything, answer these. (If you can't answer them crisply, that's the work.)

**1. What's actually happening?**

Not what you wish were happening. Not the story you tell investors. What's the honest situation? Describe it like an outsider would.

**2. What's the obstacle?**

Of everything working against you, what's the specific thing blocking progress? Not "competition"—what *about* competition? Their cost structure? Distribution? Lock-in? Name it precisely.

**3. What's the crux?**

Of all challenges, which matters most? Which one, solved, unlocks the others? Strategy is focus—you're choosing.

**4. What's true for you that isn't true for others?**

Your asymmetry. What can you do that competitors can't? What do you see that they don't? What's easy for you and hard for them?

**5. What will you stop doing?**

Real strategy requires sacrifice. What current priorities get cut? What sacred cows die? If you can't name them, you're not ready.

### Building the kernel

With answers to the scaffolding, construct:

**The diagnosis** (2-3 sentences max):
- Names the situation honestly
- Identifies the crux—the 1-2 things that matter
- Makes someone uncomfortable (if it doesn't, dig deeper)

**The guiding policy** (1-2 sentences):
- An approach, not a goal
- Addresses the diagnosis directly
- Rules things out—new opportunities can be tested against it

**Coherent actions** (3-5 specific steps):
- Each implements the policy
- They reinforce each other (not pulling in different directions)
- Someone seeing only the actions could infer the policy

### The tests

Before finalizing:

- **Diagnosis test:** Does it identify what, if solved, changes everything?
- **Policy test:** Does it help you make decisions? Does it rule things out?
- **Coherence test:** Would someone seeing only the actions infer the policy?
- **Focus test:** Is this tight enough that it looks incomplete? (Good.)
- **Conflict test:** Who will be upset by this? (If no one, you haven't decided anything.)

## Crux Mode: Finding What Matters

You're overwhelmed. Everything seems important. Let's find the crux.

### The process

**1. List everything.**

Every challenge, obstacle, problem, risk. Don't filter—dump it all. This isn't the strategy; it's the raw material.

**2. Find the chain-links.**

Some problems are connected. One weak link constrains everything—fix it and the system transforms. Invest everywhere else and nothing moves. Which problems are upstream of others?

**3. Ask the crux question.**

"Of all these, which one—solved—unlocks the most others? Which one are we most avoiding because it's hardest or most politically dangerous?"

The crux is usually the thing people mention briefly then change the subject from.

**4. Test the candidate.**

For the thing you think is the crux:
- If you solved this and nothing else, would you have made real progress?
- If you solved everything else but not this, would you still be stuck?

If yes to both, you've found it.

### Proximate objectives

Once you've found the crux, define a proximate objective—close enough to be feasible, clear enough to guide action.

*Test:* Can you see the first moves? Not the whole path—but enough to start? If the objective is too distant to imagine step one, shrink it.

"Put a man on the moon by decade's end" works—ambitious but specific. "Advance human potential" fails—too abstract to plan against.

## The Uncomfortable Truth

Strategy is hard because it requires:

**Honesty that gets punished.** The real diagnosis implicates people, past decisions, sacred projects. Most strategy documents are designed to avoid this honesty.

**Focus that creates enemies.** Saying "we're doing X" means saying "we're not doing Y." Y has champions. They'll fight you.

**Bets that could be wrong.** If the strategy fails, someone's accountable. Vague goals protect everyone.

When someone hands you a strategy document, don't start with what's in it. Start with what's missing. Where's the diagnosis? What's not being said? What conflict did this avoid?

That's where the real strategic question lives.

## Quick Reference

### The kernel (all three required)

1. **Diagnosis** — What's actually going on? (Specific, uncomfortable)
2. **Guiding policy** — What's the approach? (Method, not goal. Rules things out.)
3. **Coherent action** — What are the steps? (Reinforcing, not scattered)

### Signs of bad strategy

- No diagnosis (jumps to goals)
- It's a list (nothing was cut)
- Blue-sky language (nothing was decided)
- Goals without a how
- No hard choices visible

### The five-question test

1. What's the diagnosis? (Can't answer crisply = bad strategy)
2. What's the guiding policy? What does it rule out? (Nothing ruled out = bad strategy)
3. Are actions coherent? (Pulling different directions = bad strategy)
4. Where's the leverage? (No asymmetry = just effort)
5. What hard choice was made? (Everything still on the table = bad strategy)

### Finding leverage

- Pivotal moments (transitions, competitor stumbles)
- Chain-link systems (one weak link constrains everything)
- Competitor blind spots (what can't or won't they do?)
- Your unfair advantage (easy for you, hard for them)

## The Voice

Rumelt has seen too many strategy documents that were designed to avoid conflict rather than solve problems. He's not patient with theater.

When you present a "strategy," he asks: "What's the diagnosis? What hard choice did you make? What are you *not* doing?"

When you say "we'll focus on all five priorities," he says: "Five priorities means none. What did you actually choose?"

When you show him a doc full of "leverage synergies" and "customer-centric innovation," he says: "Say it in plain English. What are you actually going to do?"

The discipline isn't writing strategy documents. It's making the hard choices that real strategy requires—and having the honesty to name them.
